Science & Technology example article:

EU's Cresson rules out human cloning research.

The European Union opposes research into human cloning, EU Research Commissioner Edith Cresson said on Tuesday. ""For obvious ethical reasons, there is a consensus among the international scientific community to ban research into human cloning,"" Cresson told the European Parliament. Cresson, a former French socialist prime minister, noted that EU research programmes excluded human cloning and several member states had also banned such work. Reacting to the recent successful cloning of a sheep, named Dolly, at Scotland's Roslin Institute, Cresson said this raised important ethical questions about animal welfare, biodiversity and possible risks of extending such techniques to humans. Cresson said the EU's executive commission had asked its biotechnology ethics committee, an independent advisory body, to report back on the implications of the Scottish cloning by May. The most obvious danger was the temptation to use the technique, which creates genetically identical individuals by splitting embryos or by transferring the nucleus of one cell into an egg, to clone humans. ""But we should not underestimate potential benefits of such a discovery, notably for human health,"" Cresson said. Advocates of animal cloning point to other advantages, such as the possibility of producing sheep which are entirely free from scrapie, a disease similar to mad cow disease. ""There are ethical problems in using the technique. There are ethical problems in not using the techniques as well,"" said Ken Collins, chairman of the parliament's environment committee. Euro-MPs from across the political spectrum ruled out taking the technique further and cloning human beings. They are expected on Wednesday to pass a resolution demanding an immediate global ban on human cloning and a moratorium on animal cloning. ""We cannot assume that because we have something under control in the European Union, that it is universally under control. It isn't,"" Collins warned. Euro-MPs want the Commission to play a leading role in pushing for a worldwide ban on human cloning, namely by convincing Japan and the United States -- the EU's main competitors in the field of biotechnology.

Health example article:

3M says wins Memphis breast implant case.

Minnesota Mining and Manufacturing Co said on Friday that a Memphis, Tennessee jury ruled in its favor in a breast implant case. The jury decided after six hours of deliberation that Minnesota Mining should not be held liable for allegations by the plaintiff, Sandra Tyson, that her breast implants caused her to have health problems, Minnesota Mining said. The trial lasted five weeks. This is the second time in the past year that a jury has found in favor of the company, Minnesota Mining said, adding that in a third case, the jury was deadlocked. ""We are extremely pleased by the verdict,"" said Dr Carol Ley, associate medical director for the company. ""It means that science is having its day in court, with a legal decision supporting what the medical community has determined. The well-controlled scientific studies on breast implants, which number at least 16, have found no association between silicone gel breast implants and disease. ""We sympathize with anyone who is ill,"" Ley said in a news release. ""But this suit and others like it are not based on scientific evidence."" Shares of Minnesota Mining rose 3/8 to 72-1/2.
